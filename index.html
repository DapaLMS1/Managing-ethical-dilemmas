Managing Ethical Dilemmas: Principles in Conflict

Ethical dilemmas arise when two or more core ethical principles conflict, making the course of action unclear. Click on each scenario below to analyse the risks and determine the best professional practice.

A Structured Approach to Resolution

Resolving ethical dilemmas requires a systematic approach to balance conflicting duties while upholding professional standards.

  1. Identify the conflict: Clearly articulate the core issue and the ethical principles involved (e.g., autonomy vs. beneficence).
  2. Identify stakeholders: Determine who will be affected by the decision (patient, dentist, staff, practice).
  3. Identify options: List all possible courses of action.
  4. Evaluate options: Assess each option against the Codes of Conduct, practice policy, and legal requirements.
  5. Seek counsel: Consult with the supervising dentist or practice management.
  6. Act and Document: Implement the chosen course of action and meticulously document the entire process and rationale.

Dilemma: Identifying the Conflict The dental assistant observes a colleague misusing their access privileges by viewing the confidential records of a family friend, who is not their patient and is not scheduled for treatment. The primary ethical conflict here is Fidelity (loyalty to a colleague) versus Justice (upholding patient rights and legal confidentiality). The immediate decision is whether to prioritize team harmony or the patient's privacy rights.

Common Risks & Unprofessional Action

  • Ignoring the Breach: Failing to report the incident normalises unprofessional conduct and exposes the practice to serious legal penalties under the Privacy Act 1988.
  • Direct Confrontation: Directly accusing the colleague may escalate the conflict and prevent objective reporting and resolution via management.
  • Breach of Trust: The colleague has violated the "need to know" principle, which undermines patient autonomy and the ethical standard of the team.

Best Practice & Professional Duty

  • Prioritise Patient Privacy (Justice): The ethical duty to the patient and the legal requirement (Duty of Care) to maintain confidentiality outweigh loyalty to a colleague.
  • Follow Reporting Policy: Immediately report the incident, discreetly and confidentially, to the supervising dentist or practice manager.
  • Documentation: Ensure a factual, unbiased record is made of the observation, date, and time for internal compliance records.
  • Ethical Framework: Adhering to the Code of Conduct ensures the practice environment remains trustworthy and legally compliant.

Dilemma: Identifying the Conflict A patient, 'John B.', is in the chair for a root canal consultation. The dental assistant incorrectly pulls up the chart for 'John D.' and the dentist begins discussing a complex, expensive implant plan. The assistant notices the discrepancy but is reluctant to interrupt the dentist. The conflict is between the duty to provide correct information (Veracity) and the immediate duty of safety and proper process (Non-Maleficence).

Common Risks & Unprofessional Action

  • Failing to Identify Patient (Stakeholder): Continuing the discussion for the wrong chart leads to potential legal harm and confusion for John B.
  • Failing to Identify Options: The patient receives irrelevant and anxiety-inducing information, violating Beneficence (duty to help) by failing to present the correct treatment options for their actual condition (root canal).
  • Breach of Veracity: Allowing the misleading conversation to continue undermines the fundamental ethical duty to be truthful with the patient.
  • Charting Error: If the conversation is documented on the wrong chart (John D.), it creates massive administrative and legal cleanup, jeopardizing the clinical records of both patients.

Best Practice & Professional Duty

  • Act Immediately (Non-Maleficence): The assistant must interrupt the discussion immediately to prevent further confusion and potential harm.
  • Re-confirm Stakeholder Identity: Politely confirm the patient's identity (full name and DOB) verbally and correct the chart on the screen to match John B.'s file.
  • Identify Correct Options (Veracity & Beneficence): Restart the consultation using the correct chart, ensuring the dentist discusses the actual required treatment (root canal) and the appropriate options for John B..
    1. Option 1: Root canal therapy (Optimal).
    2. Option 2: Extraction (Intermediate/Alternative).
    3. Option 3: Observation/Referral (If warranted).
  • Comprehensive Documentation: Meticulously document the error, the correction, and the patient's confirmation of the correct treatment plan in John B.'s chart.

Dilemma: During a scheduled check-up, the dentist notices an urgent, unexpected issue (e.g., a cracked tooth needing immediate temporary repair). They briefly explain the procedure, but due to time constraints, the dental assistant is asked to skip the standard formal process of documenting patient consent and detailed notes before starting the emergency repair.

Common Risks & Unprofessional Action

  • Missing Consent: Performing any non-routine procedure without documented consent exposes the practice to a potential battery claim (even for minor procedures).
  • Poor Legal Defense: Lack of immediate, detailed documentation makes it impossible to defend the clinical decision or procedure outcome if the patient makes a complaint later.
  • Loss of Continuity: Subsequent practitioners relying on the incomplete record will be unable to accurately assess the patient's history or previous treatment, compromising future care quality.

Best Practice & Professional Duty

  • Prioritise Documentation of Consent: Even in an urgent scenario, the assistant must ensure the patient provides verbal consent and that this is recorded in the notes immediately before the repair starts.
  • The Golden Rule: The adage is: "If it wasn't written down, it didn't happen." The assistant must make concise, real-time notes of the findings, the reason for the urgent repair, and the consent obtained.
  • Time Management: Inform the dentist that while the emergency can be addressed, the patient must be rebooked for a later, full appointment to complete the documentation, final treatment plan, and proper note entry.
  • Ethical Duty: The duty to provide Beneficence (treating the urgent issue) must be balanced with Justice (maintaining professional standards and documentation).
